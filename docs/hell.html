While the intention behind this application might stem from a desire to improve society, the mechanics described in the text outline a platform that is practically unworkable, ethically dystopian, and ultimately useless for driving genuine social change. Here is a straightforward breakdown of why this application's design is fundamentally flawed:

1. The Ethical Nightmare of "Performative Altruism"

The core mechanic requires users to record a video as "proof" of their good deeds, particularly in the "Human Care Path" (e.g., giving food or clothes to someone in need).

Loss of Dignity: Forcing someone to be filmed receiving charity so the giver can earn "social points" strips the recipient of their dignity. It turns vulnerable people into props for someone else's digital clout.

Transactional Goodness: It conditions people to only perform civic duties if there is a camera rolling and a tangible reward waiting, rather than fostering genuine empathy.

2. A Content Moderation and Privacy Disaster

The application’s approach to privacy and security is completely backwards.

Forced Peer Review: The rule stating a user must watch and rate three videos before posting their own is a massive friction point. Worse, it turns the entire user base into unconsenting content moderators.

The Troll Factor: Because the internet is unpredictable, trolls will inevitably upload fake, offensive, or illegal content. Forcing regular users to review these videos before the system can catch them is a massive liability.

Illogical Privacy Claims: The text claims to "care about privacy" but actively tracks GPS, timezone, and records video of public and private interactions. Deleting the video after it has been reviewed by strangers does not undo the privacy violation of filming it in the first place.

3. Gamifying Basic Human Decency

The platform attempts to assign "economic value" and "coins" to everyday social interactions.

Over-complication: Rating students, teachers, colleagues, and businesses on a constant, transactional basis creates a hyper-surveilled, stress-inducing environment.

Subjective Metrics: How do you accurately "rate" someone walking a dog versus someone picking up trash? Gamifying morality always fails because social nuances cannot be reduced to a binary "positive point" system.

4. A Corporate Greenwashing Machine

The application offers "External Image" and "Public Exposition" for commercial brands that participate.

Cheap PR: It allows massive corporations to gain unearned social credit by having their employees do performative tasks (like planting a few trees on camera) while potentially ignoring massive, systemic issues within their actual business models (like labor exploitation or massive carbon footprints). It’s a tool built for corporate greenwashing.

5. Logistical Uselessness

Finally, the app is simply too cumbersome to survive in the real world. Asking people to open an app, select a path, start GPS tracking, record a video, and watch three other videos just to pick up a piece of litter is an absurd amount of bureaucracy. People will simply choose not to do it.

In short, the application fails because it tries to solve complex human and social problems with a rigid, points-based surveillance system.
